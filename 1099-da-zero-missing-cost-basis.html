1099-DA Shows Zero or Missing Cost Basis? What to Do Before You File

A zero or missing 1099-DA cost basis can make your crypto gain look higher than expected. Here is what it may mean, what records to check, and how to estimate your mismatch risk before filing.

If your 1099-DA shows zero or missing cost basis, do not panic and do not assume the form is automatically wrong. Treat it as a risk signal. The broker may have reported proceeds but not enough basis information. Before you file, gather your purchase records, wallet history, transfer records, and exchange exports so you can calculate or review your actual basis carefully.

Form 1099-DA is used by brokers to report digital asset proceeds from broker transactions and, in some cases, basis, to both taxpayers and the IRS. For many 2025 digital asset statements, the IRS says basis may not be included, so taxpayers may need to calculate basis themselves to determine gain or loss. IRS: Understanding your Form 1099-DA

Why a zero or blank cost basis matters

Cost basis is generally what you paid for the asset, adjusted where needed for fees, transaction costs, or other tax-related details. When basis is missing, your tax software may not know what you originally paid. When basis is shown as zero, the software may treat the full sale amount as potential gain unless you enter or reconcile the missing data.

That does not mean the broker lied. It also does not mean the IRS has already decided your basis is zero. It means there may be a reporting gap between what the broker knows and what actually happened across your wallets, exchanges, and transaction history.

Why this happens with crypto and digital assets

Digital asset reporting is messy because your broker may only see part of the story.

For example, an exchange may know that you sold 0.5 ETH in 2025, but it may not know where that ETH originally came from. Maybe you bought it on another exchange years ago. Maybe you moved it from a self-custody wallet. Maybe you received it from a DeFi transaction, NFT sale, gift, reward, or prior swap.

If the broker cannot confirm the original acquisition details, it may leave the basis blank or report information that does not match your own records. IRS instructions for Form 1099-DA also say that for sales after 2025, brokers must report basis for covered digital assets, but they are not required to report basis for noncovered securities. IRS: Instructions for Form 1099-DA

The key point: blank basis is not the same as zero basis. IRS instructions say brokers should leave box 1g blank in certain noncovered situations and enter -0- only if the digital asset actually had a basis of zero. IRS: Instructions for Form 1099-DA

A realistic wallet-transfer example

Imagine you bought 1 ETH on Exchange A in 2022 for $1,400 plus fees. Later, you transferred that ETH to your MetaMask wallet. In 2025, you moved 0.5 ETH from MetaMask to Exchange B and sold it for $2,000.

Exchange B may report the 2025 sale on Form 1099-DA because it handled the sale. But Exchange B may not know that you originally bought the ETH on Exchange A in 2022. From Exchange B’s view, the asset arrived from an external wallet. It may report the proceeds but leave basis blank.

If you file using only the blank basis, your software might treat the full $2,000 as gain. But if your records show that the 0.5 ETH had a real purchase cost, your gain calculation may be different. That is why missing basis should trigger review, not panic.

Records to gather before you file

Before accepting a zero or missing basis result, gather as much evidence as possible. Start with:

  • 1099-DA statements from each broker or exchange
  • Exchange CSV exports for buys, sells, swaps, deposits, and withdrawals
  • Wallet transaction history from MetaMask, Ledger, Coinbase Wallet, Trust Wallet, or other wallets
  • Purchase confirmations and trade receipts
  • Transfer records between wallets and exchanges
  • Deposit and withdrawal timestamps
  • Transaction hashes for on-chain movements
  • Gas fee records where relevant
  • Prior-year tax reports if the asset was acquired before 2025
  • Crypto tax software reports, if used
  • Notes about gifts, mining, staking, airdrops, rewards, or business payments
  • Screenshots as backup, not your only proof

The goal is not to force a number. The goal is to reconstruct a reasonable, supportable record trail before filing.

Common mistakes to avoid

1. Assuming blank basis means zero basis

A blank basis often means the broker did not have enough information to report basis. It does not automatically mean your asset cost nothing.

2. Assuming the 1099-DA is always wrong

A zero or missing basis is a warning sign, not automatic proof of broker error. Sometimes the form may be correct. Sometimes it may be incomplete. Sometimes your own records may also be incomplete.

3. Filing before checking wallet transfers

Wallet-to-exchange transfers are one of the most common reasons basis gets lost. If you moved assets between platforms, do not rely only on the selling exchange.

4. Ignoring fees

Fees may affect your final gain or loss calculation depending on the transaction and how your records are prepared. Do not delete fee data from your exports.

5. Mixing up proceeds and gain

Proceeds are what you received from the sale or disposition. Gain or loss generally depends on proceeds minus basis, after considering the correct rules and adjustments. A 1099-DA may show proceeds without showing the full tax result.

6. Waiting until filing day

The longer you wait, the harder it becomes to retrieve exports, match wallet transactions, and review transfer history calmly.

What to do next

Start with a simple triage process. First, compare the 1099-DA proceeds against your exchange activity. Check whether the sale amount looks reasonable. Then look at the basis field. Is it blank? Is it zero? Does the form indicate whether basis was reported to the IRS?

Next, trace where the asset came from. If it came from the same exchange where it was sold, your records may be easier. If it came from another exchange or wallet, you need to connect the earlier purchase or acquisition record to the later sale.

Then use the 1099-DA Zero or Missing Cost Basis Calculator to estimate how much risk the missing basis creates. If the possible overstatement is small, your next step may be basic record cleanup. If the possible overstatement is large, you may need a deeper reconciliation or professional review.

FAQ

Does a zero basis on Form 1099-DA mean I owe tax on the full sale amount?

Not automatically. A zero basis should not be assumed unless the asset actually had a zero basis. If you paid for the asset or acquired it in a way that created basis, you may need to reconstruct your records before filing.

Why is my 1099-DA missing cost basis?

Your broker may not know your original purchase price, especially if the asset was transferred in from another exchange, wallet, or DeFi platform. For many 2025 digital asset statements, the IRS says basis may not be included, so taxpayers may need to calculate it from their own records. IRS digital asset reminders

Should I contact the exchange if the basis looks wrong?

You can check the exchange’s tax center, download full transaction exports, and review whether a corrected form process exists. But do not rely only on customer support. Your own records may still be needed, especially for assets that moved across wallets or platforms.

Can crypto tax software fix missing basis automatically?

Crypto tax software can help, but it depends on the data you give it. If you do not import the original purchase exchange, self-custody wallet, or transfer history, the software may still show missing or zero basis. Software is a tool, not a substitute for complete records.

What should I do if I cannot find the original purchase record?

Gather the best available records: exchange exports, bank payment records, wallet history, transaction hashes, prior tax reports, and platform emails. Then consider getting help from a qualified tax professional. Do not guess casually, and do not assume zero unless the facts support it.
